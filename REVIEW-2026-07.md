# Wealth Dashboard — review, July 2026

Where it stands: 18 feature tabs, 51 pure core engines, 666 core + 13 UI
tests, ~11k lines of UI. The engine layer is the strongest part of this
codebase — genuinely tested, honest about its assumptions, and mostly
free of the "plausible number with no provenance" problem that afflicts
consumer finance tools. The weaknesses are now concentrated in the UI
layer and in a few structural gaps.

Ranked by value ÷ effort. Impact is on *your* use, not a hypothetical
market.

---

## Tier 1 — do these next

### 1. Deleting anything is instant and unrecoverable
**Impact: high · Effort: low**

`TwoStepDelete` exists and is used in 6 tabs. But Budget, Income,
Pension, Property, RSU and Private all still have one-click trash icons
that drop a row immediately, with no confirm and no undo. One misclick
in the ledger silently changes your CGT position; one in Budget removes
a transaction from a year's spend total.

The fix is not more confirm dialogs — it's an **undo toast**: delete
immediately (fast, no friction), show "Deleted X · Undo" for ~8 seconds,
restore on click. One shared component, applied everywhere. This is the
single highest-value UI change available.

### 2. There is no "am I on track?" comparison
**Impact: high · Effort: medium**

You have daily net-worth snapshots and a deterministic projection. You
have never compared them. `PlanHealthCard` shows the plan's own verdict;
it does not ask whether reality is following it.

A chart of **actual net worth against the plan's projected path**, with
the divergence in £ and %, answers the question the whole app exists to
answer. Everything needed is already computed — this is a joining
exercise, not new modelling. It's also the natural home for a
`plan-drift` action-queue item alongside the `spend-drift` one.

### 3. Savings rate is computable and absent
**Impact: medium-high · Effort: low**

You now know income (salary, dividends, interest) and spending (Budget).
Savings rate — the single most predictive number in accumulation — is a
subtraction you're not doing. Show it on Home as a trailing-12m figure
with a trend, and in Plan as a cross-check against the contribution
assumptions (`empPct`/`erPct`), which are currently asserted rather than
verified against what actually leaves your account.

### 4. PlanTab is a 2,840-line screen with 10 sub-tabs
**Impact: medium · Effort: medium**

Overview, Accumulation, Decumulation, Income floor, Run-off, Buy-to-let,
Drawdown, Stress, Adequacy, IHT. Ten peer-level tabs means nothing is
prominent and the file is unmaintainable. Suggested regrouping:

- **Plan** (overview + accumulation + decumulation) — the projection
- **Resilience** (floor, run-off, stress, adequacy) — "what if"
- **Estate & property** (IHT, BTL)
- Drawdown stays inside Decumulation as a strategy panel

Split the file to match. Worth doing before adding anything else there.

## Tier 2 — high value, more work

### 5. Household mode (never built — roadmap 2.2)
**Impact: high if applicable · Effort: high**

If there's a partner, the app is modelling half your position and giving
materially wrong answers on: two ISA allowances, two CGT annual exempt
amounts, inter-spouse transfers (the standard way to use both AEAs),
the transferable nil-rate band and RNRB, and joint retirement timing.
The tax engines already take the parameters; the data model and UI
don't. This is the largest single gap in correctness — but only if it
applies to you.

### 6. Open Banking (skipped — roadmap 3.4)
**Impact: medium-high · Effort: high, plus ongoing cost**

Would eliminate statement CSV downloads and make the Budget tab
self-maintaining. TrueLayer/Plaid both have UK coverage. The honest
counter-argument: it introduces a third party holding read access to
your accounts, recurring cost, and consent re-authorisation every 90
days. Manual CSV import is unglamorous but sovereign. I'd defer this.

### 7. Data health, in one place
**Impact: medium · Effort: low-medium**

Unpriced holdings, stale prices, unledgered RSU vests, missing ERI
entries, uncategorised spend, un-reconciled statement gaps, stale broker
imports — each currently nags in its own tab. A single **Data health**
panel (probably on Home, collapsed) with a completeness score and a
jump-to-fix per item would turn seven scattered warnings into one
maintenance ritual.

### 8. Scenario comparison, side by side
**Impact: medium · Effort: medium**

You can save and load scenarios; you can't see two at once. "Retire at
57 vs 60" is the question people actually ask, and answering it
currently means loading one, memorising the numbers, loading the other.
A two-column diff of key outcomes (success rate, first-sale year, estate,
lifetime tax) would make the scenario library worth having.

## Tier 3 — polish and smaller gaps

### 9. Accessibility is thin outside three tabs
PlanTab has 2 aria attributes across 2,840 lines. Charts have no text
alternative; the Segmented/pill controls are div-based in places.
Keyboard navigation works for tables (SortTh got real buttons) but not
for the sub-tab bars. Not urgent for single-user software, but it's
also the kind of debt that only grows.

### 10. Mobile is untested
Every table is wrapped in `overflow-x-auto`, which is the right
primitive, but a 10-column run-off table on a phone is a horizontal
scroll marathon. If you ever check this on a train, the Home tab and
Budget Overview are the two screens worth making genuinely responsive
(card layouts under `sm:`), and the rest can stay scroll-and-zoom.

### 11. The run-off ignores tax on withdrawals
The run-off nets RSU/deferred comp correctly, but portfolio *sales* to
cover a shortfall are shown gross. In a GIA those disposals generate CGT,
so the real sale needed is larger than shown. The CGT engine already
exists — wiring an estimate in would remove a known optimistic bias.

### 12. Budget: no month-on-month comparison
The Overview shows a period in isolation. A "vs previous period" or "vs
12-month average" column would turn a snapshot into a trend, which is
what actually tells you a category is drifting.

### 13. Income tab and Budget tab don't meet
Income (investment income in) and Budget (spending out) are adjacent
concepts on separate screens with no cross-reference. At minimum, the
Budget's trailing-12m spend and the Income tab's trailing-12m receipts
belong on one line somewhere: "you received £X, you spent £Y".

### 14. Export beyond backup
There's a JSON backup and an AI snapshot. There's no CSV export of the
ledger, no printable/PDF tax summary for the SA108 work, and no
year-end pack. If an accountant ever needs to see this, everything is
currently trapped in the browser.

## What NOT to do

- **Don't add more tabs.** The IA is at capacity; the next feature
  should displace or absorb something.
- **Don't re-attempt AI categorisation via the serverless route** until
  Vercel's env-var behaviour is resolved. The paste-to-Claude flow gets
  the same result with no infrastructure.
- **Don't chase real-time price streaming.** Daily prices are correct
  for every decision this app supports; live ticking would add cost and
  encourage exactly the behaviour the tool is designed to discourage.

## Honest assessment of the foundations

The parts that are genuinely good and worth protecting:

- **Pure, tested engines with stated assumptions.** Almost every module
  documents what it deliberately does *not* model. That's rare and it's
  why the numbers can be trusted.
- **Refusal to fabricate.** `planSpendFromBudget` returning `ready:
  false` with reasons, the run-off disclosing its dividend circularity,
  the XIRR scope fix — the app repeatedly chooses "no answer" over "a
  confident wrong answer".
- **Single sources of truth.** `PERSIST_KEYS`, backup generation from
  it, `mergedSpend` — with exhaustiveness tests that fail when someone
  forgets. The `mergedSpend` extraction happened *because* three copies
  diverged; that instinct is the right one.

The main structural risk is UI-layer sprawl: PlanTab and CgtSection are
now large enough that changes there are riskier than changes to any
engine, and the UI test suite (13 smoke tests) is thin relative to 666
engine tests. If anything gets systematic attention next, it should be
that asymmetry.
